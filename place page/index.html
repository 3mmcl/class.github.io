GEOGRAPHY

READ MORE

The city is surprisingly small for its dense population, only extending as much as 6 miles (10km) from the square in front of the Notre-Dame Cathedral with rivers cutting through the spaces. Their banks, as well as the city streets and stone walls, are lush with trees, ivy, and other greenery to give the city a garden-esc feel that settles it nicely in the vast forests that surround it.

PEOPLE

READ MORE

Paris is a densely packed, diverse city whose population boomed from 1850, reaching a peak of 2.9 million, before steadily declining after 1931 as families moved to nearby towns around the capital instead. Most of its population are originally from outside the city, with a large portion of immigrants, mostly those from countries affiliated with the European Union.

Parisians have garnered the stereotype of being snobbish, stuck-up, or downright rude, but testimony from some people online attribute it to a clash of culture and social expectations, as well as the general busyness of being a metropolitan city.

ECONOMY

READ MORE

Paris is a major commerce centre filled with opportunity for both national and international businesses. Traditionally, its industries focused mainly on luxury goods, but through the construction of canals and railways, it expanded to heavier industries. The Seine remains both a tourist attraction and prominent barge traffic to move downstream to ports such as Rouen and Le Havre.

Because most major French banks and other financial institutes are primarily located in Paris, finance has also become an important part of Paris’ economy alongside other services such as conventions and shopping centres. The modern department store was first invented by the French and many are scattered all throughout the busy city streets and more suburban areas alike.
